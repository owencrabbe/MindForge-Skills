# Steelman and Red Team

The cheapest place to discover a fatal flaw is here, in private, before a
competitor, a reviewer, an investor, or reality discovers it for you. Agreeable
feedback feels good and teaches nothing. This skill exists to do the opposite of
flattery: to make an idea genuinely stronger by first making it as strong as it
can be, then trying as hard as possible to destroy it.

The two-phase structure matters. If you attack an idea before understanding its
strongest form, you defeat a strawman and learn nothing. If you only steelman,
you fall in love and stay blind. Do both, in order.

## Phase 1: Steelman

Build the strongest, most charitable, most competent version of the position
under examination, even stronger than the user articulated it. This is not
agreement. It is a stress-test requirement: an attack only counts if it defeats
the *best* version of the idea, so you have to construct that best version
first.

- State the core claim in its most defensible form.
- Supply the best available evidence and reasoning for it, including support the
  user did not think to mention.
- Articulate the strongest motivation: what would have to be true for this to be
  not just correct but obviously correct?

If you cannot make the idea sound genuinely compelling here, either the idea is
very weak or you do not yet understand it. Do not move on until the steelman is
something a smart proponent would proudly sign.

## Phase 2: Red team

Now switch sides completely and attack the steelmanned version as a motivated,
intelligent adversary would. Not nitpicks. The attacks that actually matter.
Work through these angles and keep the ones with teeth:

- **The load-bearing assumption.** What single assumption, if false, collapses
  the whole thing? How likely is it to be false?
- **Disconfirming evidence.** What facts, data, or precedents cut against it?
  What would the smartest opponent cite?
- **Failure modes.** How does this break in practice? What is the realistic bad
  scenario, not the worst-case fantasy but the plausible one?
- **Second-order effects.** If this works, what does it cause downstream that
  bites back? Who is incentivized to fight it?
- **The base rate.** How often do things in this reference class actually
  succeed? Is the user assuming they are the exception, and why?
- **Who disagrees and why.** Smart people who reject this position hold what
  view, and what do they see that the proponent does not?
- **The uncomfortable question.** The one the user is avoiding. Ask it plainly.

Rank the attacks by how much damage they do. A single fatal flaw outweighs ten
cosmetic ones. Be specific: "the CAC assumption ignores that your channel
saturates at ~5k users" beats "the growth assumptions seem optimistic."

## Phase 3: Verdict and repair

Critique without a path forward is just discouragement. After the assault,
render an honest judgment and show what survives.

- **What survives:** the parts of the idea that withstood the attack. These are
  real strengths, now battle-tested.
- **What breaks:** the flaws serious enough to require a change of plan.
- **The verdict:** Does the idea survive in its current form, survive with
  modification, or fail? Say it plainly.
- **The repair:** How to fix or de-risk the surviving flaws. For each fatal
  attack, either a mitigation or an honest "this may be a dealbreaker."

## Output format

```
## Steelman: the strongest version
[The best case for the position, stated so well a proponent would endorse it]

## Red team: the strongest attacks
1. [Most damaging attack] — why it hurts, how likely, how bad
2. [Next] ...
(ordered by severity)

## Verdict
[Survives / survives with changes / fails] — and the honest reasoning

## What survives and what to fix
- Survives: [battle-tested strengths]
- Must fix: [flaw] → [specific mitigation]
- Possible dealbreaker: [flaw with no easy fix, named honestly]
```

## The standard

The user asked for this because they want the truth, not comfort. Deliver it.
The kindest thing you can do for someone about to make a big bet is to attack
their thinking harder than the world will. But stay fair: the red team must hit
the steelman, not a caricature. An attack that only works against a weak version
of the idea is a waste of everyone's time. Real strength on both sides is what
makes the exercise worth doing.
