# First-Principles Thinking

Most reasoning is analogy in disguise. People decide what to do by looking at
what is already being done and adjusting slightly. That is efficient and it is
also how entire industries stay trapped in local optima for decades. This skill
does the opposite: it boils a problem down to the few things that are actually,
physically, economically true, and reconstructs the answer from those alone.
The payoff is the occasional insight that a whole field has been leaving on the
table because "that's how it's done."

## The method

### 1. State the problem and the received answer

Write down what the user is trying to do and the conventional approach to it.
Naming the default explicitly is important, because the goal is to separate what
is necessary from what is merely inherited. You cannot strip away an assumption
you have not made visible.

### 2. Decompose to bedrock

Keep asking "why is that true?" and "what is that actually made of?" until you
hit things that cannot be reduced further without denying physics, math,
verified data, or defined terms. Bedrock facts are the ones where the honest
answer to "why?" is "because that is a law of nature" or "because that is what
the number is" or "because that is what the word means."

For each element of the conventional approach, sort it into one of two piles:

- **Bedrock:** genuinely irreducible. A rocket must reach orbital velocity. A
  loan must be repaid or defaulted. A protein folds according to its sequence.
- **Inherited:** true only because someone decided it, or because it was true
  once, or because everyone copies everyone. "Batteries cost $600/kWh" was
  treated as bedrock until someone priced the raw materials and found they cost
  a fraction of that. The gap was inherited assumption.

Be ruthless here. Most things people treat as fixed constraints are inherited.
Cost structures, timelines, "the way the market works," "what customers expect,"
regulatory interpretations, org charts. Interrogate each one: is this a law, or
a habit?

### 3. Find the load-bearing assumption

Among the inherited items, one or two are usually doing the real damage. These
are the assumptions that, if false, change everything downstream. Name them
explicitly. This is the highest-leverage output of the whole exercise, because
an assumption you can see is an assumption you can test or discard.

### 4. Rebuild from bedrock

Now reconstruct a solution using only the bedrock facts as constraints, ignoring
the inherited ones. Ask: if I were solving this for the first time, knowing only
what is truly required, what would I build? The convention may turn out to be
right after all, and if so you now understand *why* it is right rather than just
copying it. But often a cheaper, faster, or entirely different path appears once
the inherited walls are gone.

### 5. Pressure-test the reconstruction

A first-principles answer that ignores a real constraint is just a fantasy. Go
back through the bedrock list and confirm the new approach actually satisfies
every irreducible requirement. Then identify what it would cost to test the
load-bearing assumption cheaply, before betting everything on it.

## Output format

```
## The problem and the default approach
[What we're solving, and how it's conventionally done]

## Decomposition
Bedrock truths (irreducible):
- [fact] — why it's irreducible
Inherited assumptions (only true by convention/habit/history):
- [assumption] — where it came from, whether it still holds

## The load-bearing assumption
[The one or two assumptions that, if false, change everything. This is the
crux.]

## Rebuilt from first principles
[The solution constructed from bedrock alone. May match convention, may not.
Explain the reasoning either way.]

## The cheapest way to test the crux
[How to falsify the load-bearing assumption for as little time/money as
possible before committing.]
```

## Where this earns its keep

The mistake to avoid is performing first-principles theater: fancy language
wrapped around the conventional answer. The exercise only matters if you are
genuinely willing to discover that the default is wrong. So actively hunt for
the version of the problem where the convention breaks. When the user is
intimidated by a cost, a timeline, or a "you can't do that," that is precisely
the moment this skill is most valuable, because those barriers are inherited far
more often than they are bedrock. Help the user see which wall is real and which
is painted on.
